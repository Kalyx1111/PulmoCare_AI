Pulmonology

Chest & Lung Conditions

Comprehensive clinical research from GOLD, GINA, ATS/ERS, NICE and NTEP guidelines.

⚠
All information is for educational research. Respiratory conditions require clinical examination, spirometry, and imaging. Never delay assessment for severe breathlessness or coughing up blood.

Quick Clinical Reference

Life-Threatening Asthma Signs

  • Silent chest, cyanosis, feeble respiratory effort
  • Bradycardia, hypotension, exhaustion, confusion
  • Peak flow under 33% predicted
  • Rising/normal CO2 is an ominous sign, NOT reassurance

TB Screening Red Flag

  • Cough >2-3 weeks is the key symptom prompting investigation
  • CBNAAT/Truenat first-line rapid molecular test per NTEP
  • Detects rifampicin resistance simultaneously
  • NTEP provides free diagnosis and treatment nationwide

Lung Cancer Red Flags

  • Cough >3 weeks, especially new/changed in a smoker
  • Haemoptysis always requires investigation
  • Unexplained weight loss, hoarseness, finger clubbing
  • Urgent CXR/CT referral, particularly if smoker over 40

India Air Pollution & COPD

  • Biomass fuel smoke is a major under-recognised COPD cause
  • Disproportionately affects women who cook with solid fuels
  • Ambient air pollution also drives asthma exacerbations
  • Clean cooking fuel access programmes aim to reduce exposure
Reversible Airway Disease

Asthma

Research on GINA stepwise management and acute exacerbation from GINA guidelines.

Diagnosis and Control

Wheeze, breathlessness, chest tightness, cough (often nocturnal or exercise-triggered). Diagnosis requires demonstrated variable airflow limitation via spirometry with reversibility testing.

Control assessed by symptom frequency, reliever use, night waking, activity limitation. Poor control should prompt review of inhaler technique and adherence before escalating therapy.

GINA Stepwise Management

All adults/adolescents should receive ICS-containing therapy rather than SABA-only reliever - SABA-only treatment increases exacerbation risk.

Track 1 (preferred)

As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol at all steps, escalating to regular ICS-formoterol maintenance plus as-needed at higher steps.

Add-on for Severe Asthma

LABA, LTRA, LAMA, or biologics (omalizumab for allergic, mepolizumab/benralizumab/dupilumab for eosinophilic phenotype) guided by phenotyping.

Acute Severe Asthma

Life-threatening features: silent chest, cyanosis, bradycardia/hypotension, exhaustion, confusion, peak flow under 33% predicted.

Management

High-flow oxygen, nebulised salbutamol (repeated/continuous) plus ipratropium, systemic corticosteroids early, IV magnesium sulphate for severe/life-threatening not responding to initial bronchodilators. Escalate to intensive care if deteriorating.

Progressive Airflow Limitation

COPD

Research on GOLD assessment and management from GOLD guidelines.

Definition and Assessment

Persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation, usually from noxious particle/gas exposure. Biomass fuel smoke is a major and under-recognised cause in India, particularly affecting women. GOLD ABE tool combines symptom burden with exacerbation history to guide initial therapy.

Management

Smoking cessation is the single most important intervention. Bronchodilators (LAMA/LABA combination) are mainstay. ICS added for frequent exacerbators with elevated eosinophils - avoid ICS monotherapy given pneumonia risk. Pulmonary rehabilitation strongly evidenced but underutilised. Long-term oxygen for chronic severe hypoxaemia improves survival.

Exacerbations

Acute worsening requiring treatment escalation: increased bronchodilators, short-course systemic corticosteroids, antibiotics if increased sputum purulence, controlled oxygen (target 88-92% if hypercapnic risk), NIV for acute hypercapnic respiratory failure with acidosis.

India Priority

Tuberculosis

Research on TB diagnosis, treatment, and India's NTEP programme.

India Burden

India carries the world's highest TB burden. NTEP coordinates control efforts with free diagnosis (including CBNAAT/Truenat rapid molecular testing) and treatment nationwide. Drug-resistant TB (MDR/XDR) is a major ongoing challenge requiring specialised prolonged regimens.

Presentation and Diagnosis

Chronic cough >2-3 weeks (key symptom prompting investigation), fever, night sweats, weight loss, haemoptysis. HIV co-infection alters presentation (more extrapulmonary, smear-negative).

CBNAAT/GeneXpert now first-line: rapid, detects rifampicin resistance. Culture remains gold-standard sensitivity but slow. Chest X-ray supportive, not diagnostic alone.

Treatment

Standard regimen: 2 months HRZE (isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) intensive phase, then 4 months HR continuation - daily dosing throughout. Monitor for hepatotoxicity (isoniazid/rifampicin/pyrazinamide) and visual changes (ethambutol - optic neuritis risk). Drug-resistant TB requires newer WHO-recommended shorter all-oral regimens (bedaquiline, linezolid) under specialist supervision.

Interstitial Lung Disease

Research on IPF, connective tissue disease-associated ILD, and occupational lung disease.

Overview and Diagnosis

Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF): most common and generally most aggressive idiopathic form. HRCT chest central to diagnosis; multidisciplinary discussion (pulmonologist, radiologist, pathologist) considered best practice.

Treatment

IPF: antifibrotic agents (pirfenidone, nintedanib) slow progression but do not reverse fibrosis. CTD-associated ILD: immunosuppression may be appropriate, contrasting with IPF where it's generally not beneficial.

Occupational Lung Disease in India

Silicosis and coal workers' pneumoconiosis remain prevalent in mining, stone-cutting, and construction workforces, often under-recognised given limited occupational health surveillance. Silicosis notably significantly increases TB risk.

Respiratory Infections

Respiratory Infections

Research on pneumonia and bronchitis from ATS/ERS guidelines.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

CURB-65 score guides site-of-care decisions. Typical organisms: S. pneumoniae most common; atypical organisms need different antibiotic coverage. Vaccination reduces incidence in high-risk groups.

Acute Bronchitis

Usually viral, self-limiting. Antibiotics generally not indicated without underlying lung disease or pneumonia signs - overuse is a significant driver of antimicrobial resistance.

Early Detection

Lung Cancer

Research on red flag symptoms, diagnosis, and management from ATS/ERS guidelines.

Red Flag Symptoms

Persistent cough >3 weeks (especially new/changed in a smoker), haemoptysis, unexplained weight loss, persistent chest/shoulder pain, hoarseness, finger clubbing. Any in a current/former smoker over 40 warrants urgent investigation.

Diagnosis and Management

CT chest for staging; PET-CT for mediastinal/distant assessment. Molecular testing (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, PD-L1) standard for advanced NSCLC to guide targeted therapy/immunotherapy. EGFR-mutant adenocarcinoma more common in Asian never-smokers including India.

Pleural Space

Pleural Disease

Research on pleural effusion and pneumothorax.

Pleural Effusion

Light's criteria distinguish transudate (treat underlying cause) from exudate (broader workup including ADA for TB in endemic settings like India). Empyema requires prompt drainage plus antibiotics.

Pneumothorax

Primary (young, thin, no underlying disease) vs secondary (underlying lung disease, higher risk). Tension pneumothorax is an EMERGENCY requiring immediate needle decompression - do not await imaging if clinically suspected.

Pulmonary Emergencies

🚨
PULMONARY EMERGENCIES — 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US): Acute severe asthma, tension pneumothorax, massive haemoptysis, ARDS, massive PE. ALL LIFE-THREATENING.

🚨 Acute Severe Asthma

Silent chest, cyanosis, exhaustion, peak flow under 33%.

  • Immediate high-flow oxygen, continuous nebulised bronchodilators
  • Systemic corticosteroids, IV magnesium sulphate
  • Rising/normal CO2 is ominous, not reassuring

🚨 Tension Pneumothorax

Progressive chest pain/breathlessness, hypotension, tracheal deviation.

  • IMMEDIATE needle decompression
  • Do not wait for chest X-ray confirmation if clinically suspected
  • Followed by formal chest drain insertion

🚨 Massive Haemoptysis

Large-volume coughing up of blood, risk of asphyxiation.

  • Position with bleeding side dependent if known
  • Urgent bronchoscopy/CT angiography to localise source
  • Bronchial artery embolisation often definitive treatment

🚨 ARDS

Acute severe hypoxaemic respiratory failure with bilateral infiltrates.

  • Requires intensive care management
  • Lung-protective ventilation (low tidal volume)
  • Prone positioning for moderate-severe cases
India Context

Pulmonology in India

India-specific air pollution burden, TB elimination programme, and occupational lung disease.

Air Pollution Burden

India experiences among the highest ambient and household air pollution levels globally, a major contributor to COPD, asthma exacerbations, and respiratory infections. Household air pollution from biomass cooking disproportionately affects women and children.

TB Elimination Programme

NTEP coordinates India's TB control strategy, providing free diagnosis (rapid molecular testing) and treatment nationwide, alongside nutritional support (Ni-kshay Poshan Yojana) and digital adherence tools. India has an ambitious TB elimination target ahead of the global WHO timeline.

Occupational Lung Disease

Silicosis and pneumoconioses remain significant in mining, quarrying, and construction sectors, often affecting informally employed workers with limited occupational health monitoring or compensation access.
